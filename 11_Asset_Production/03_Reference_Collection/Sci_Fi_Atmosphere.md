# Sci-Fi Atmosphere

Sci-fi without the costume. We borrow the **feeling** of advanced systems,
not the visual tropes.

---

## What to learn

- **Quiet futurism.** The future doesn't shout. It hums.
- **Material restraint.** Glass, brushed metal, ceramic, fabric. No chrome.
- **Atmospheric thickness.** Haze, depth, distance — air has weight.
- **One impossible thing per frame.** Everything else is plausible.
- **Earth tones can be sci-fi.** See Dune. See Foundation. See Arrival.

---

## What to avoid (the cyberpunk trap)

- Neon pink + neon teal = instant cliché
- Holograms with grid lines and "scanning" effects
- Floating UI with too many panels
- Rain on glass at night (we've all seen it)
- Anime-influenced AI faces
- "Hacker" green text on black
- Glitch overlays as personality
- RGB on anything

If it could be a Cyberpunk 2077 still, we don't ship it.

---

## What fits SheetFlow

- **Foundation-grade interiors** — pale, lit, calm, important.
- **Arrival-grade alien-but-warm** — unfamiliar geometry, familiar light.
- **Apple Vision Pro reveal frames** — premium future, not dystopian future.
- **Devs (FX, 2020)** — algorithms made beautiful through restraint.
- **2001: A Space Odyssey interior shots** — the original quiet future.

## Atmosphere recipe (use this)

```
Base:        deep neutral (graphite #14181C → ink #0A0D10)
Accent 1:    cool sapphire highlight (#3D5A80 at 18% opacity)
Accent 2:    warm amber rim (#D4A76A at 6% opacity)
Haze:        4–6% volumetric, neutral
Bloom:       0. Use real volumetric light instead.
Color contrast: temperature only, not hue
```

---

## Cinematic observations

- The future feels expensive when surfaces look **owned**, not new.
- Imperfection (a smudge, a wear mark) sells believability.
- Sound design teaches lighting: low hum > screaming alarm.
- Skin must remain warm even in cold scenes. Never let humans go blue.
- Sci-fi that ages well looks 30 years old already. Don't chase trends.
